Deprescribing in Palliative Care

Deprescribing is the planned reduction or stopping of medications that may no longer provide meaningful benefit, may cause harm, or no longer align with a patient’s goals of care.

Why deprescribe?

In patients with limited prognosis, many preventative medications have a time-to-benefit that exceeds life expectancy. Continuing these treatments can increase pill burden, adverse drug reactions, treatment complexity, and medication-related harm.

Key principles

  • Review whether the medicine offers short-term symptomatic benefit.
  • Consider whether the medication aligns with the patient’s current goals of care.
  • Assess whether the time-to-benefit exceeds prognosis.
  • Prioritise comfort, function, and minimising medication burden.

OncPal guideline: common medications of limited benefit

The OncPal deprescribing guideline highlights several drug classes that may have limited benefit in advanced cancer and palliative care settings, depending on indication and prognosis.

Examples commonly considered for review:
Statins, fibrates, ezetimibe, antihypertensives in mild-moderate hypertension, proton pump inhibitors without clear indication, oral hypoglycaemics used for prevention of long-term complications, osteoporosis medications, vitamins, minerals, and complementary therapies.

Anticholinergic Burden (ACB): Why it matters?

Anticholinergic burden refers to the cumulative effect of taking one or more medicines with anticholinergic activity.

Why it matters in palliative care

Anticholinergic medications may worsen:

  • Delirium
  • Cognitive impairment
  • Falls risk
  • Constipation
  • Urinary retention
  • Dry mouth and sedation

How ACB scoring works

  • Score 1 – possible anticholinergic effects
  • Score 2 – definite anticholinergic effects
  • Score 3 – marked anticholinergic effects
Clinical significance: a total ACB score of 3 or more is generally considered clinically significant and is associated with increased risk of confusion, falls, and poorer functional outcomes.

Common examples

  • Amitriptyline – 3
  • Oxybutynin – 3
  • Diphenhydramine – 3
  • Lorazepam – 1
  • Morphine – 1
